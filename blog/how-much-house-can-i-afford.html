The Core Affordability Formula: 4 Keys to Your Budget

At its heart, home affordability isn't a mystery. It's a calculation based on four key financial pillars. Understanding each one is the first step to gaining control over your home search.

  • Your Income

    This is the foundation. Lenders look at your gross (pre-tax) monthly income to determine the baseline for what you can borrow.

  • Your Monthly Debts

    This includes car payments, student loans, credit card debt, and any other fixed monthly obligations. The lower your existing debt, the more you can allocate to a mortgage.

  • The Interest Rate & Term

    The current mortgage rates and the length of your loan (e.g., 15 or 30 years) directly impact the size of your monthly payment and, therefore, how much you can afford to borrow overall.

Key Factors That Influence Your Buying Power

Beyond the basic formula, several other critical elements determine your final home loan eligibility and affordability.

  • Down Payment

    The amount you pay upfront. A larger down payment (ideally 20% or more) reduces your loan amount, lowers your monthly payment, and helps you avoid Private Mortgage Insurance (PMI).

  • Credit Score

    A higher credit score signals to lenders that you are a reliable borrower, unlocking better interest rates. Even a small rate reduction can save you tens of thousands over the life of the loan.

  • Debt-to-Income (DTI) Ratio

    This is the most critical factor. It's your total monthly debts divided by your gross monthly income. Most lenders prefer a total DTI of 36% or less, though some may go up to 43%.

3 Tips to Increase Your Buying Power

If your affordability number is lower than you'd like, don't despair. You have the power to change it. Here are three effective strategies:

  • 1. Reduce Your DTI: Pay down high-interest debt like credit cards and personal loans. This single action can significantly boost your home loan eligibility.
  • 2. Improve Your Credit Score: Make all payments on time and keep your credit utilization low. A better score means a lower interest rate.
  • 3. Increase Your Down Payment: Continue saving aggressively. A larger down payment reduces the amount you need to borrow and demonstrates financial stability to lenders.

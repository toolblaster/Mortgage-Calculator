The 20% Down Rule: Myth vs. Reality

For decades, the standard advice has been to put 20% down. The primary reason for this is to avoid Private Mortgage Insurance (PMI) on a conventional loan. PMI is an insurance policy that protects the lender if you default, and it adds an extra cost to your monthly payment.

However, for many first-time buyers, saving up 20%—which is $80,000 on a $400,000 home—is the biggest barrier to homeownership. The reality is, the average down payment is often much lower, especially for first-time buyers.

Down Payment Options for Less Than 20%

Good news! You don't need a 20% down payment to buy a home. Several loan programs are specifically designed to help people get into homes with less cash upfront.

  • FHA Loans: Backed by the government, these loans allow for a down payment as low as 3.5%. This is a very popular FHA loan down payment option for first-time buyers.
  • Conventional 97 Loans: Some conventional loans allow a down payment of just 3%, making them competitive with FHA loans.
  • VA & USDA Loans: For eligible veterans and homebuyers in designated rural areas, these government-backed loans may require 0% down.

The Power of a Larger Down Payment: How It Helps You

While not always necessary, aiming for a larger down payment has powerful financial benefits that can save you a lot of money in the long run.

  • Eliminate or Reduce PMI: This is the big one. Putting down 20% on a conventional loan means you how to avoid PMI from the start, saving you a significant amount each month.
  • Lower Monthly Payment: A larger down payment means a smaller loan amount, which directly translates to a lower monthly principal and interest payment.
  • Better Interest Rate: Lenders see a larger down payment as less risky, and they may reward you with a lower interest rate, saving you thousands over the life of the loan.

How to Save for a House Down Payment

Once you have a target, it's time to start saving for a house down payment. It requires discipline, but it's achievable with a clear plan.

  • Set a Clear Goal: Determine your target home price and calculate your desired down payment amount.
  • Automate Savings: Set up automatic transfers to a dedicated high-yield savings account each payday.
  • Track & Cut Spending: Use a budgeting app to identify areas where you can cut back, like subscriptions or dining out.
  • Look for Assistance: Research state and local first-time buyer down payment assistance programs.

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

1

Is it OK to put less than 20% down?

Yes, it is very common. Millions of homeowners buy homes with less than 20% down using options like FHA loans (3.5% minimum) or conventional loans (3-5% minimum). The main drawback is that you will likely have to pay Private Mortgage Insurance (PMI).

2

How much is the minimum down payment for a house?

The minimum depends on the loan type. For eligible borrowers, VA and USDA loans can be 0% down. FHA loans require as little as 3.5%. Some conventional loans for first-time buyers have minimums as low as 3%.

3

Does a higher down payment lower your interest rate?

Often, yes. A larger down payment reduces the lender's risk, which can make you eligible for a lower interest rate. A lower rate can save you a significant amount of money over the life of the loan.

4

How does my credit score affect down payment requirements?

A higher credit score makes you a more attractive borrower and can help you qualify for low down payment programs. For some loans, like FHA, there are minimum credit score requirements (typically around 580) to be eligible for the low 3.5% down payment option.

5

What is PMI, and when can I remove it?

Private Mortgage Insurance (PMI) is an insurance policy that protects the lender if you default on your loan, required when you put down less than 20% on a conventional loan. You can typically request to have PMI removed once your loan-to-value (LTV) ratio reaches 80%, meaning you have 20% equity in your home.

A Homeowner's Guide to Property Taxes

What is Property Tax?

Property tax is a tax paid on property owned by an individual or other legal entity, such as a corporation. It is a primary source of revenue for local governments, funding essential public services that benefit the community.

  • Public Services: Funds schools, police and fire departments, libraries, and road maintenance.
  • Based on Value: The amount you pay is based on the assessed value of your property, which is determined by your local government's tax assessor.

Key Terms to Understand

  • Assessed Value vs. Market Value: Market value is what your home could sell for. Assessed value is a percentage of that value used by tax authorities. For example, a $400,000 home might have an assessed value of $320,000 (80%). Taxes are paid on the assessed value.
  • Mill Rate: A "mill" is one-thousandth of a dollar. A mill rate is the tax per $1,000 of assessed value. A rate of 25 mills means you pay $25 for every $1,000 of your home's assessed value. This is equivalent to a 2.5% tax rate.
  • Effective Tax Rate: This is the percentage of your home's market value that you pay in property taxes each year. Our calculator uses this rate for its state-based estimates, making it easy to compare locations.

Frequently Asked Questions

Market value is the price your home would likely sell for on the open market. Assessed value is a percentage of the market value, determined by your local tax assessor for tax purposes. Property taxes are calculated based on the assessed value.

A mill rate is a tax rate expressed in mills, where one mill is one-tenth of a cent ($0.001). It represents the amount of tax payable per dollar of the assessed value of a property. For example, a mill rate of 10 means you pay $10 for every $1,000 of assessed value, which is equivalent to a 1% tax rate.

Property taxes are a primary source of funding for local services like schools, police, fire departments, and infrastructure. States and municipalities have different funding needs and other revenue sources (like income or sales tax), which causes property tax rates to vary significantly.

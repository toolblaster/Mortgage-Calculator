Understanding Home Affordability: A Simple Guide

Figuring out "how much house can I afford" is the most important first step in the home-buying journey. It’s not just about the maximum loan a bank will give you; it’s about finding a monthly payment that fits comfortably in your budget. This guide breaks down the key factors.

The Core Components of Affordability

  • Your Income: This is the foundation. Lenders look at your stable, gross (pre-tax) monthly income.
  • Your Debts: Lenders add up all your monthly debt payments (car loans, credit cards, student loans) to see how much of your income is already spoken for.

The Most Important Metric: Debt-to-Income (DTI) Ratio

Your DTI ratio is your total monthly debt payments divided by your gross monthly income. Most lenders use two types:

  • Front-End DTI (Housing Ratio): Your proposed new housing payment (PITI). Lenders like to see this at 28% or less.
  • Back-End DTI (Total Debt Ratio): Your proposed housing payment PLUS all your other monthly debts. Lenders generally want this to be 36% or less, though some loans allow up to 43%.

Our calculator uses the back-end DTI for its primary calculation, as it provides the most realistic picture of what a lender will approve.

How to Improve Your Affordability

If the number is lower than you'd like, don't worry! You can take steps to increase your buying power:

  • Reduce Debt: Paying off a high-interest credit card or car loan can significantly lower your DTI ratio and free up borrowing capacity.
  • Boost Your Credit Score: A higher credit score qualifies you for lower interest rates, which means your monthly payment will be smaller for the same loan amount, effectively increasing your affordability.

Frequently Asked Questions

1

What is the 28/36 rule for affordability?

The 28/36 rule is a guideline used by lenders. It suggests that your total housing costs (PITI) should not exceed 28% of your gross monthly income (front-end DTI), and your total debt payments (including housing) should not exceed 36% of your gross monthly income (back-end DTI).

2

Does my down payment affect how much I can afford?

Yes, significantly. A larger down payment reduces the amount of money you need to borrow, which in turn lowers your monthly mortgage payment. This makes it easier to qualify for a loan and stay within your desired DTI ratio.

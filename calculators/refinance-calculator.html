Should I Refinance My Mortgage? A Guide to Making the Right Choice

Refinancing your mortgage can be a powerful financial move, potentially saving you thousands of dollars. But it's not the right choice for everyone. This guide explains what to look for and how to use our refinance calculator to see if it makes sense for you.

Top Reasons to Consider Refinancing

  • Lower Your Interest Rate: This is the most common reason. If current market rates are significantly lower than your existing rate (often by 0.75% or more), you can reduce your monthly payment and total interest paid.
  • Shorten Your Loan Term: Refinancing from a 30-year to a 15-year loan will increase your monthly payment but can save you a massive amount in interest and help you build equity much faster.
  • Tap Into Home Equity: A "cash-out" refinance allows you to borrow against the equity you've built, providing funds for home renovations, debt consolidation, or other large expenses.

The Key Metric: The Break-Even Point

Refinancing isn't free. You'll have to pay closing costs, just like you did with your original mortgage. The most important calculation is your break-even point.

  • This is the point in time when your accumulated monthly savings equal the upfront closing costs. Our calculator figures this out for you instantly. If you plan to stay in your home longer than the break-even point, refinancing is likely a good financial move.

Frequently Asked Questions

1

Does refinancing hurt your credit score?

Refinancing involves a hard credit inquiry, which can cause a small, temporary dip in your credit score. However, for most people with good credit, the score recovers within a few months. The long-term benefits of a lower interest rate often far outweigh the minor, short-term impact on your credit.

2

What is a 'no-cost' refinance?

A 'no-cost' refinance means you don't pay the closing costs out of pocket. Instead, the lender covers them in one of two ways: by charging a slightly higher interest rate, or by rolling the closing costs into the new loan principal. It offers convenience, but it's important to calculate if it's truly the best financial option long-term.
